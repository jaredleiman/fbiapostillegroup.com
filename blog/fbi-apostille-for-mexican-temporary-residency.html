FBI Apostille for Mexican Temporary Residency

FBI Apostille GroupMay 20269 min read

Mexico has become one of the top destinations for American retirees, remote workers, and long-term expats. The temporary residency visa — Residente Temporal — is the standard entry point for Americans planning to stay longer than 180 days. It requires an apostilled FBI background check, and the rules around that document are specific enough that getting it wrong is one of the most common application errors.

This guide covers what Mexican consulates require, how the freshness window works, and how to fit the FBI step into the broader residency application timeline.

Why Mexico Requires an FBI Apostille

Mexico's immigration law requires foreign nationals applying for temporary or permanent residency to demonstrate they do not have a disqualifying criminal history. For US applicants, this means submitting a federal-level background check — the FBI Identity History Summary — authenticated with a Hague Apostille for use in Mexico.

Mexico is a signatory to the Hague Convention, so it accepts documents authenticated through the apostille process rather than through consular legalization. The apostille on the FBI document tells Mexican immigration authorities that the document is a genuine US federal record.

Which Visa Categories Require It

The FBI apostille requirement applies to the temporary residency visa and, when converting to permanent residency after four years, to the permanent residency application as well. It does not apply to tourist permits (FMM) or to visitor visas for non-immigration purposes.

What Mexican Consulates Require

The Document

The FBI Identity History Summary is the required document. It must come from the FBI directly or through an FBI-authorized channeler. Third-party background check services that pull from commercial databases are not accepted. The document must reflect the applicant's full federal criminal history.

The Apostille

The apostille must come from the US Department of State. The FBI background check is a federal document, which means state Secretaries of State do not have authority to issue apostilles for it. A state-level apostille on an FBI document will be rejected. The Department of State apostille is the only valid authentication for federal documents under the Hague Convention.

Freshness Window

Mexican consulates generally require the FBI background check to be issued within the last six months. Some consulates have applied a three-month window in practice. The controlling date is the FBI issuance date on the document — not the date the apostille was attached. An FBI document issued 7 months ago with an apostille issued last week will still be rejected at a consulate enforcing the six-month rule.

Consulate Variation

Mexican consulates in the United States vary in how strictly they apply the freshness rule. The consulates in Los Angeles, Houston, Chicago, and New York process the highest volume of residency applications from Americans. All require the FBI apostille; the freshness window varies from three to six months depending on the office. When in doubt, treat three months as the safe standard.

Translation

Mexican consulates in the United States process the FBI apostille in English and generally do not require a Spanish translation for the initial temporary residency application. The immigration office in Mexico — the Instituto Nacional de Migración (INM) — may request a Spanish translation when the applicant arrives in Mexico to complete the residency card process. Requirements vary by state and office.

The Full Temporary Residency Document Checklist

The FBI apostille is one component of the temporary residency application. The full checklist includes:

  • Completed visa application form
  • Valid US passport with at least six months of validity and blank pages
  • Two recent passport photos (specific size requirements vary by consulate)
  • Proof of economic solvency (bank statements, pension documentation, investment accounts, or rental income records meeting the INM income thresholds)
  • Apostilled FBI background check (issued within 3 to 6 months)
  • Application fee payment

The economic solvency requirement is the other document that frequently causes delays. Mexico's INM sets minimum monthly income or savings thresholds that change periodically and vary between temporary and permanent residency categories. Applicants should verify current thresholds with the consulate at the time of application.

How Long the FBI Apostille Takes

The FBI apostille process involves two sequential steps: the FBI Identity History Summary and the Department of State apostille.

FBI Identity History Summary2–4 weeks2–4 weeks
Department of State Apostille6–8 weeks3–5 weeks
Total8–12 weeks5–9 weeks

For applicants targeting a consulate appointment, the FBI step should be initiated 10 to 12 weeks before the appointment date to allow full processing time on the expedited track while keeping the document within the freshness window. Initiating the FBI step more than 14 weeks before the appointment risks the document aging past the six-month freshness threshold.

Applying as a Couple

Both spouses applying for temporary residency require their own apostilled FBI background checks. The applications are processed as separate individuals at the consulate — each requires a complete document packet including an individual FBI apostille. The economic solvency documentation can be combined for joint applications at some consulates, but the background check requirement is always per-person.

Converting to Permanent Residency

After four years of temporary residency, Americans are eligible to convert to permanent residency (Residente Permanente). This conversion is processed by the INM inside Mexico, not at a consulate. Background check requirements for the conversion vary. Some INM offices require an updated FBI apostille; others accept the applicant's existing residency history without a new background check. Applicants should contact their local INM office well in advance to confirm current requirements.

Common Mistakes

Using a state background check. Only the federal FBI Identity History Summary is accepted. State police background checks, even from the applicant's state of residence, do not satisfy the requirement.

Getting a state-level apostille on the FBI document. The FBI document is federal. The Department of State is the only authority that can apostille it. State-level apostilles on FBI documents will be rejected.

Timing the FBI step too early. Applicants who pull the FBI document at the start of a multi-month visa planning process frequently find the document has aged past the consulate's freshness window by the time of their appointment.

Forgetting the economic solvency documentation. The FBI apostille and the financial documentation both need to be current. Applicants who time the FBI step correctly but neglect to prepare current bank statements often find their financial documentation is also stale by appointment day.
