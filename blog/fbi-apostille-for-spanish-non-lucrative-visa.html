FBI Apostille for Spanish Non-Lucrative Visa

FBI Apostille GroupMay 20269 min read

Spain's Non-Lucrative Visa is the primary legal pathway for Americans who want to live in Spain without working — retirees, remote workers earning income from foreign sources, and people living off savings or investments. The application has a fixed document checklist, and the FBI background check apostille is one of the non-negotiable items on it. Getting it wrong is one of the most common reasons NLV applications are rejected or delayed at Spanish consulates.

This guide covers what the Spanish NLV requires for the FBI apostille, how the consulates enforce the rules, and how to sequence the document correctly.

Why Spain Requires an FBI Apostille for the NLV

The Non-Lucrative Visa is a long-stay visa that grants the right to reside in Spain for one year, renewable in two-year increments thereafter. Because it leads to legal residency, Spanish authorities require a background check that covers the applicant's full criminal history at the national level — not just the state where they currently live.

The FBI Identity History Summary is the only document that satisfies this requirement for US applicants. A state police background check, even one from the applicant's home state, is not accepted. The apostille from the US Department of State authenticates the FBI document for use in Spain.

What Spanish Consulates Require

The Document

The FBI Identity History Summary — the document commonly called an "FBI background check" — must be the federal-level document issued directly by the FBI's Criminal Justice Information Services Division. Third-party background check services that aggregate data are not accepted. The document must come from the FBI itself, either through a direct request or through a channeler authorized by the FBI.

The Apostille

The apostille must come from the US Department of State, not from a state Secretary of State. The FBI document is a federal record, so it requires federal authentication. State-level apostilles on FBI documents are invalid and will be rejected by the consulate.

Freshness Window

Spanish consulates typically require the FBI background check to be issued within the last three months of the visa application date. This is one of the stricter freshness windows in the European visa context. Three months from the FBI issuance date — not the apostille date — is the controlling timeframe.

Critical: Three-Month Window

The three-month freshness rule is enforced at the consulate appointment. If your FBI document was issued more than 90 days before your appointment date, it will be rejected regardless of how recently it was apostilled. Plan accordingly.

Translation

Spanish consulates in the United States generally do not require translation of the FBI apostille for the NLV application. The document is submitted in English. However, if the applicant later applies for residency renewal inside Spain, some regional immigration offices have requested Spanish translations at that stage. For the initial NLV application at a US consulate, translation is typically not required.

The Full NLV Document Checklist

The FBI apostille sits alongside a set of other required documents for the NLV application. The full list includes:

  • Completed NLV application form (modelo nacional)
  • Valid US passport with at least one year of validity remaining
  • Two recent passport photos
  • Proof of sufficient financial means (bank statements, pension documentation, investment accounts)
  • Private health insurance covering Spain with no copays and repatriation coverage — from a provider operating in Spain
  • Apostilled FBI background check (issued within 3 months)
  • Medical certificate signed by a licensed physician
  • Completed application fee payment

Each consulate in the US handles NLV applications independently and has some discretion in how it interprets the requirements. The consulates in Miami, Los Angeles, New York, Houston, and Chicago are the highest-volume offices for American NLV applicants. All require the apostilled FBI background check.

The FBI apostille involves two steps: the FBI Identity History Summary request and the Department of State apostille. The total timeline depends on which service levels are used.

Given the three-month freshness window, the total processing time has to fit within the 90 days before the consulate appointment. This is the binding constraint for NLV applicants: the FBI document must be issued within 90 days of the appointment, and the apostille itself takes most of that time to complete.

Recommended Sequencing

Submit the FBI fingerprint card no later than 10 weeks before your scheduled consulate appointment. This gives enough time for the FBI processing, expedited State Department apostille, and a small buffer. Do not submit fingerprints more than 12 to 13 weeks before the appointment or the document may age past the three-month window.

Applying for the NLV as a Couple or Family

When two spouses apply for NLV together, each adult requires their own apostilled FBI background check. The financial means requirement for joint applications is higher than for a single applicant, and both applicants must independently satisfy the background check requirement.

Minor children do not require background checks. However, they do require the other supporting documents (passport, application form, photos, medical certificate) and must be included in the application.

Renewals and the Background Check

The initial NLV is valid for one year. After entering Spain, applicants apply for a Tarjeta de Identidad de Extranjero (TIE) — the physical residency card. The first renewal, typically at the two-year mark, is processed by local immigration offices in Spain.

Background check requirements for NLV renewals vary by region. Some provincial immigration offices require an updated background check. The document for Spanish use at this stage may require a Spanish sworn translation. If the renewal process runs inside Spain, the apostille is still required to authenticate the FBI document for official use.

Common Mistakes

Using a state background check. State police background checks are not accepted for the NLV. Only the federal FBI Identity History Summary satisfies the requirement.

Starting too late. Applicants who begin the FBI step less than 8 weeks before their consulate appointment frequently find the document arrives past the appointment date, requiring a new appointment.

Starting too early. Applicants who pull the FBI document 4 to 5 months before their appointment find it is past the three-month freshness window by the time of the appointment. The FBI step has to be timed precisely.

Using a state Secretary of State for the apostille. The FBI document is federal. The apostille must come from the US Department of State. State-level apostilles on federal documents are not valid.
